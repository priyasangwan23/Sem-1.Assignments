Mutual funds in India

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia — The first introduction of a mutual fund in India occurred in 1963, when the Government of India launched the Unit Trust of India (UTI).[1] Mutual funds are broadly categorised into three segments: equity funds, hybrid funds, and debt funds.

Mutual fund statistics

  • The total AUM of the Indian mutual fund industry as of December 31, 2023 stood at ₹50.78 trillion (US$600 billion).This is a significant milestone, marking over a six-fold increase compared to the ₹8.26 trillion (US$98 billion) recorded in December 2013[2]
  • According to SEBI, during FY 2022–23, 73% of mutual fund units were redeemed within 2 years of investment; only 3% continued for more than 5 years.[3][4]
  • According to the Reserve Bank of India report, mutual funds attracted 6% of household savings in FY2023 while less than 1% went into direct equities.Almost 95% of household savings in India park their money in bank deposits, including fixed deposit, provident fund, PPF, life insurance, and various small savings schemes.[5][6][7]
  • According to the SPIVA reports and other studies show a majority of actively managed funds underperform benchmarks over long periods.[8][9]

Controversies

The Indian mutual fund industry has faced several crises and controversies over the years, often related to credit defaults, liquidity squeezes, and governance issues.

List of Mutual fund companies/schemes bankrupted, defaulted or closed

2020 Franklin Templeton Mutual Fund fiasco

In April 2020, Franklin Templeton India unexpectedly wound up six credit funds with assets close to US$4 billion, citing lack of liquidity amid the coronavirus pandemic. SEBI investigated and found lapses and violations; subsequent regulatory action included a temporary ban on launching new debt schemes and penalties. The winding up led to panic withdrawals across some credit funds and wide media attention.[15][16][17][18]

Reliance Mutual Fund

In 2019, debt schemes of Reliance Mutual Fund faced liquidity stress following exposures to troubled companies such as DHFL, leading to heavy redemptions and forced asset sales.[19][20]

IL&FS crisis and impact

The IL&FS defaults of 2018 caused significant markdowns in NAVs for funds holding its securities and prompted regulatory changes such as issuer limits and improved risk management expectations.

Amtek Auto Impact

Several funds with exposure to Amtek Auto faced problems after the company's default in 2015, forcing suspended redemptions and other protective measures in certain funds.

Birla Sun Life Mutual Fund (Aditya Birla Sun Life Mutual Fund)

In 2018, redemption pressures affected some debt schemes due to exposure to Essel Group entities — reported large exposures led to investor concerns and scrutiny.

Dewan Housing Finance Corporation (DHFL) crisis and impact

DHFL's defaults in 2019 created a severe liquidity crunch for several mutual funds that had exposure to its debt, which in turn led to downgrades, write-downs and tightened regulations.

2001 UTI Mutual Fund (Unit Trust of India) fiasco

The 2001 UTI crisis (US-64) triggered government intervention and restructuring of UTI due to large redemption pressures and market manipulation events associated with the Ketan Parekh scam.

DHFL Pramerica Mutual Fund

Joint-venture funds and fund houses with exposure to DHFL's debt were forced to mark down values and faced governance and liquidity challenges.

Yes Mutual Fund

The Yes Bank crisis in 2020 impacted mutual funds with exposure to the bank's debt papers; several schemes recorded write-downs and investor losses.

Market segment

The mutual fund market in India consists of fund houses, distributors, investors, and the regulator (SEBI). Products span equity, debt, hybrid, ETF and index funds.
